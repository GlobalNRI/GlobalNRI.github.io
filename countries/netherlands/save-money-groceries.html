How to Save Money on Groceries in the Netherlands

This guide explains practical and realistic ways to reduce your grocery bill in the Netherlands. It covers affordable supermarkets, discount strategies, loyalty programs, weekly offers, local markets, and smart shopping habits—especially useful for expats and new residents.

1. Choose the right supermarket

Not all supermarkets are priced the same. Your choice of store has the biggest impact on grocery spending.

  • Discount supermarkets: Aldi and Lidl are usually the cheapest for basics.
  • Mid-range: Jumbo and Albert Heijn offer convenience, quality, and frequent promotions.
  • Ethnic stores: Turkish, Asian, and Indian stores often sell vegetables, rice, lentils, and spices at lower prices.

2. Buy supermarket private-label brands

Most Dutch supermarkets have high-quality store brands that are much cheaper than name brands.

  • Albert Heijn: AH Basic and AH Huismerk
  • Jumbo: Jumbo Huismerk
  • Lidl & Aldi: Mostly private-label products by default

3. Use weekly discounts and bonus offers

Weekly promotions are a major source of savings.

  • Albert Heijn Bonus offers (requires free Bonus Card).
  • Jumbo weekly deals and multi-buy discounts.
  • Check supermarket apps or flyers before shopping.

4. Get loyalty cards and supermarket apps

Loyalty programs are free and essential for saving money.

  • Albert Heijn Bonus Card (digital or physical).
  • Jumbo Extra’s loyalty program.
  • Lidl Plus app for exclusive discounts and coupons.

5. Shop at local markets (weekmarkt)

Open-air markets can be cheaper, especially for fresh produce.

  • Vegetables and fruits are often cheaper than supermarkets.
  • Visit near closing time for additional discounts.
  • Markets usually operate once or twice a week in each neighborhood.

6. Reduce food waste with discount apps

Several apps help you buy surplus food at a fraction of the price.

  • Too Good To Go: Discounted food boxes from supermarkets and bakeries.
  • Olivia / Foodello: Discounted long-shelf-life products.

7. Buy in bulk (but selectively)

Bulk buying saves money for non-perishable items.

  • Rice, pasta, flour, lentils, and canned goods.
  • Toilet paper and household essentials during promotions.
  • Avoid bulk buying fresh items unless you can freeze them.

8. Cook seasonally and plan meals

Seasonal produce is cheaper and fresher.

  • Plan weekly meals before shopping.
  • Base meals around discounted items.
  • Freeze leftovers to avoid waste.

9. Compare prices per unit

Dutch price labels show cost per kilogram or liter.

  • Always compare the €/kg or €/L price.
  • Smaller packages are often more expensive per unit.

10. Example monthly savings

By combining discount supermarkets, weekly offers, loyalty apps, and meal planning, a household can often reduce grocery spending by 20–35% compared to shopping without planning.

11. Practical tips for expats

  • Carry reusable shopping bags—plastic bags cost extra.
  • Self-checkout lanes are common and faster.
  • Most supermarkets close early on Sundays, except in big cities.
