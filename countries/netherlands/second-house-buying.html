Buying a Second House in the Netherlands

Practical guidance for non-residents and residents considering a second home—holiday, investment, or future retirement property.

Overview

Purchasing a second home in the Netherlands is possible for both residents and non-residents. Second homes include holiday homes, investment/rental properties, and secondary residences. Rules, taxes and financing differ from buying a primary home—this page summarizes the most important points and steps to take.

Key Legal & Tax Considerations

  • Transfer tax and other purchase costs: Buying a property incurs transfer tax and notary fees. Rates and amounts vary depending on whether the property is used as a primary residence or not; consult a notary for current rates and exact costs.
  • Property taxes and municipal charges: Ongoing costs include municipal property tax (OZB), water board charges and waste/solids fees. Some municipalities charge a tourist tax when you rent short-term.
  • For flats: Apartments are usually part of an owners' association (VvE). Check the VvE's financials, reserve fund and rules before buying.
  • Energy and inspections: Properties must have an energy label; organising an inspection (bouwkundige keuring) is recommended before purchase.

Financing & Mortgages

Mortgages for second homes are available but often come with stricter lending conditions: lower loan-to-value, higher interest rates, and additional documentation. Non-residents can obtain mortgages in the Netherlands, but lenders typically require more proof of income and may limit the loan amount.

Speak with a mortgage adviser (hypotheekadviseur) early to get pre-approval and to understand tax implications (e.g., mortgage interest deduction rules usually apply only to primary residences).

Buying for Short-Term Rental or Investment

  • Local rules: Municipalities set rules for short-term rentals (Airbnb-style). Some cities restrict or require permits—check the local municipality regulations.
  • Tourist tax and administration: If you rent to tourists you will likely collect tourist tax and must register guests in accordance with local rules.
  • Insurance & management: Insure the property for rental use, and consider a local property manager if you don’t live nearby.

Practical Buying Steps

  1. Set a budget including taxes, notary and refurbishment costs.
  2. Research locations and local rules (tourist permits, VvE status, rental demand).
  3. Contact a local real estate agent (makelaar) and a mortgage adviser.
  4. Visit properties and order a technical inspection before committing.
  5. Negotiate and sign the purchase agreement; hire a notary for transfer and deed.
  6. Complete utilities, insurance and any registration (for rentals or tenants).

Ongoing Costs & Responsibilities

Budget for regular costs: insurance, property tax, utilities, maintenance, and VvE contributions for apartments. If renting out, also budget for cleaning, management fees and any local taxes.

Checklist

  • Confirm transfer tax, notary and purchase costs.
  • Review VvE documents (if applicable).
  • Obtain a building inspection and energy label.
  • Get mortgage pre-approval from a lender.
  • Check local short-term rental rules and permits.
  • Arrange appropriate insurance for second-home use.

Next Steps & Contacts

If you want, contact a local makelaar, a notary (notaris) and a mortgage adviser for tailored guidance. They can provide up-to-date tax rates, fees and municipal rules for the location you are considering.

Summary

Buying a second home in the Netherlands requires preparation and careful local checks. Use professional advice where needed and verify all legal and financial steps before completing a purchase.
