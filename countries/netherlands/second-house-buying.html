Buying a Second House in the Netherlands

Practical guidance for non-residents and residents considering a second home—holiday, investment, or future retirement property.

Overview

Purchasing a second home in the Netherlands is possible for both residents and non-residents. Second homes include holiday homes, investment/rental properties, and secondary residences. Rules, taxes and financing differ from buying a primary home—this page summarizes the most important points and steps to take.

For example, a family from India might buy a small bungalow in Zeeland for €250,000 as a holiday retreat, or an NRI investor might purchase a 2‑bed apartment near Utrecht for €350,000 intending to rent it out. The process is similar but the financial implications (tax, mortgage terms, rental rules) vary depending on the intended use.

Key Legal & Tax Considerations

  • Transfer tax and other purchase costs: Buying a property incurs transfer tax (2–8% of the price, depending on use), notary fees (€1,000–€2,000) and agent fees. For instance, a €300,000 investment flat may include €6,000 transfer tax if not used as a main residence.
  • Property taxes and municipal charges: Ongoing costs include municipal property tax (OZB, typically 0.1–0.4% of the cadastral value), water board charges and waste/solids fees. Some municipalities levy a tourist tax of €1–€5 per night for short-term rentals.
  • For flats: Apartments are usually part of an owners' association (VvE). Check the VvE's financials, reserve fund and rules before buying. A poorly funded VvE can mean surprise contributions of €2,000+ for repairs.
  • Energy and inspections: Properties must have an energy label; organising a building inspection (bouwkundige keuring) is recommended before purchase. A label F or G may reduce resale value by 5–10%.

Financing & Mortgages

Mortgages for second homes are available but often come with stricter lending conditions: lower loan-to-value (typically 70–80% rather than 90%), higher interest rates (0.25–0.75% additional margin), and additional documentation. Non-residents can obtain mortgages in the Netherlands, but lenders typically require more proof of income and may cap the loan at €500,000 or less.

Speak with a mortgage adviser (hypotheekadviseur) early to get pre-approval and to understand tax implications (e.g., mortgage interest deduction rules usually apply only to primary residences). For example, borrowing €200,000 at 4% fixed for 20 years results in a monthly payment around €1,200; without interest deduction the effective cost may be €200 higher per month.

Example Scenarios

To make things concrete, consider these typical cases:

  • Holiday home in Zeeland: Purchase price €250,000. Transfer tax ~€6,250; annual costs €2,500–€3,500 (insurance, utilities, property tax). Used a few weeks each year, it’s mainly a lifestyle purchase.
  • Rental apartment near Utrecht: €350,000 purchase, rented long-term for €1,600/month. Expect rental income €19,200/year; after mortgage and taxes net is €3,000–€5,000 annually depending on leverage.
  • Future retirement house: €450,000 recent purchase in a smaller town. Finance with a conservative mortgage, keep it empty or rent seasonally; value expected to appreciate with inflation.

Buying for Short-Term Rental or Investment

  • Local rules: Municipalities set rules for short-term rentals (Airbnb-style). Some cities restrict or require permits—check the local municipality regulations.
  • Tourist tax and administration: If you rent to tourists you will likely collect tourist tax and must register guests in accordance with local rules.
  • Insurance & management: Insure the property for rental use, and consider a local property manager if you don’t live nearby.

Practical Buying Steps

When you start the process, think of it as two parallel tracks: research (locations, rules, cost) and paperwork (finance, legal, inspections). Follow these steps in order:

  • Budget: Work out a full figure that includes purchase price, transfer tax (up to 8%), notary and agent fees, plus 5–10% for any renovations or furnishings.
  • Location study: Check municipal rules for second homes or rentals, VvE health, and typical prices. Look at rental demand if you intend to let the property.
  • Advisors: Speak to a local makelaar, hypotheekadviseur and, if necessary, a tax specialist. Getting pre-approval for a mortgage early lets you act quickly when you find a suitable property.
  • Viewings & inspections: Visit multiple properties and always order a bouwkundige keuring (building inspection). Don’t buy unseen—many NRIs rely on a local friend or agent to inspect on their behalf.
  • Offer & agreement: Negotiate the price; once both parties agree, sign the purchase agreement (koopovereenkomst). The notary will handle the transfer deed and registration.
  • After closing: Arrange insurance (home and liability), set up utilities in your name, and register with the municipality if you plan to rent. Keep good records for future tax returns.

Ongoing Costs & Responsibilities

Once you own the property it continues to cost money every month. Budget for regular costs: insurance, property tax, utilities, maintenance, and VvE contributions for apartments. If renting out, also budget for cleaning, management fees and any local taxes such as tourist levies.

Checklist

  • Confirm transfer tax, notary and purchase costs.
  • Review VvE documents (if applicable).
  • Obtain a building inspection and energy label.
  • Get mortgage pre-approval from a lender.
  • Check local short-term rental rules and permits.
  • Arrange appropriate insurance for second-home use.

Next Steps & Contacts

If you want, contact a local makelaar, a notary (notaris) and a mortgage adviser for tailored guidance. They can provide up-to-date tax rates, fees and municipal rules for the location you are considering.

Summary

Buying a second home in the Netherlands requires preparation and careful local checks. Use professional advice where needed and verify all legal and financial steps before completing a purchase.
