Netherlands Visa — Detailed Guide

The Netherlands offers multiple residence and work routes depending on your purpose: highly skilled workers, EU Blue Card holders, regular work permits, graduates seeking an orientation year, start-ups, students, family reunification, and long-term residency. Below is a concise breakdown of the most common visa types, how to apply, typical validity, salary guidance and common document requirements. Rules, fees and salary thresholds change frequently — always verify the latest requirements with the Dutch Immigration Service (IND): ind.nl/en.

Highly Skilled Migrant (HSM)

  • Who: Skilled employees hired by a recognized sponsor (employer).
  • How to apply: Employer (recognized sponsor) usually applies to IND for a residence permit on your behalf. If you need an MVV (entry visa) apply at Dutch embassy/consulate before travel.
  • Validity: Typically issued for 1 to 5 years and renewable while employed.
  • Minimum salary: Varies by age and year and is updated annually. Approx ranges (for guidance only): senior threshold ~€4,500–€5,500 gross/month; lower thresholds apply for younger applicants and recent graduates. Employer must meet threshold — check IND for current figures.
  • Documents: Passport, employment contract, salary details, employer sponsorship proof, recent photos, health insurance, birth certificate if requested.

EU Blue Card

  • Who: Highly qualified non-EU nationals with a valid employment contract and salary above the national threshold for Blue Card.
  • How to apply: Employer or employee applies to IND; you may need an MVV depending on nationality.
  • Validity: Typically 1–4 years depending on the contract; renewable.
  • Minimum salary: Higher than HSM in most years — usually indexed to national average salary. Check IND for the current annual threshold.
  • Documents: Diploma/qualification proof, employment contract, passport, passport photos, health insurance.

Work Permit (TWV) & Single Permit (GVVA)

  • Who: Workers employed by Dutch companies that cannot fill the role from the EU/EEA workforce.
  • How to apply: Employer applies for a TWV (work permit) at the Employee Insurance Agency (UWV) for short-term roles; for combined residence and work (GVVA) the employer applies at IND.
  • Validity: Typically up to 1 year for TWV; GVVA follows the length of the contract and can be extended.
  • Minimum salary: No single national threshold — depends on position, contract and labor market rules.
  • Documents: Contract, employer declarations, passport, proof employer searched EU market (for TWV), health insurance.

Orientation Year for Graduates

  • Who: Recent graduates from Dutch higher education or certain top foreign universities (check IND eligibility lists).
  • How to apply: Apply for the orientation year residence permit after graduation (or while your study permit is still valid in some cases).
  • Validity: Usually 12 months to search for work or start a business; during this period you can work without an employer sponsor.
  • Documents: Degree certificate/transcript, passport, proof of graduation, health insurance.

Start-up Visa

  • Who: Entrepreneurs who want to start an innovative business in the Netherlands under the supervision of an approved facilitator.
  • How to apply: Facilitator applies to IND or you submit with facilitator support; initial permit usually for 1 year.
  • Validity: Up to 1 year; pathway to regular residence if business grows.
  • Documents: Business plan, facilitator letter, passport, proof funds.

Student Visa (MVV & Residence Permit)

  • Who: Non-EU students enrolled at recognised Dutch education institutions.
  • How to apply: Institution often arranges the MVV (entry visa) and residence permit application with IND on your behalf.
  • Validity: For duration of study; extensions possible for subsequent studies or orientation year.
  • Documents: Proof of enrolment, passport, sufficient funds, health insurance.

Family Reunification / Family Formation

  • Who: Spouses, registered partners, children, and certain other family members of residents/citizens.
  • How to apply: Resident sponsor applies to IND for a residence permit for the family member; MVV may be required depending on nationality.
  • Validity: Varies; depends on sponsor's permit and relationship stability.
  • Documents: Marriage/civil partnership certificates, birth certificates, sponsor's residence documents, housing proof, income statements.

Permanent Residence / Long-term Residency

  • Who: Non-EU nationals who have legally and continuously lived in the Netherlands for typically 5 years (some exceptions apply).
  • How to apply: Apply to IND for permanent residence after meeting continuous residence, integration and lawful-stay criteria.
  • Validity: Permanent residence permits are long-term; conditions apply for renewal if temporary residency was granted later.
  • Documents: Proof of continuous residence, income, housing, integration (civic integration test), passport.

How to apply — common steps

  1. Identify the correct permit type (employer-sponsor routes vs. independent routes).
  2. Employer or sponsor applies as required (many work routes are employer-led).
  3. If required, apply for an MVV (entry visa) at the Dutch embassy/consulate in your country.
  4. Submit documents: passport, photos, employment contract/enrolment, proof of income, diplomas, insurance.
  5. Pay IND application fees and await decision; some routes require a TB test or additional checks.
  6. If approved, register with your municipality, get BSN (citizen service number), and arrange health insurance.

Processing times, fees and practical notes

  • Processing times: Vary by permit type and complexity; employer-sponsored residence permits and single permits are often faster than family or PR applications. Expect weeks to several months.
  • Fees: IND fees differ by permit type and nationality; consult ind.nl for current fees.
  • Salary thresholds & rules: Salary thresholds and minimums are updated yearly. Use IND as authoritative source for up-to-date figures.
  • Language & integration: Permanent residency and naturalisation routes typically require integration tests or language knowledge.

Helpful resources
