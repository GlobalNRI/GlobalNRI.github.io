Parenting & Childcare — Netherlands

A practical, step-by-step guide for new parents: pregnancy, birth, healthcare, childcare, schooling and costs.

Before You Arrive: Pregnancy & Birth Planning

  • Register with a midwife (verloskundige) early: If you are pregnant, book an appointment as soon as you arrive — prenatal care is midwife-led for low-risk pregnancies.
  • Health insurance: Dutch basic insurance covers maternity care after you become resident. If arriving pregnant, get private coverage or check with insurer about emergency coverage until BSN/insurance active.
  • Hospital vs. home birth: Many choose midwife-assisted home births; discuss options with your midwife and check hospital facilities for C-section or high-risk care.
  • Language support: Request an interpreter or English-speaking midwife if needed; many practices in major cities provide English services.

At Birth — Practical Steps

  1. Obtain a birth certificate (geboorteakte) from the municipality where the child is born — required for passport and BSN.
  2. Register the child with the municipality to receive a BSN for the baby (usually within days).
  3. Apply for child benefits (kinderbijslag) and parental leave payments through the Sociale Verzekeringsbank (SVB) if eligible.

Postnatal Care & Vaccinations

  • Youth healthcare (Jeugdgezondheidszorg): Free check-ups and vaccinations through JGZ; enrol your child once you have a BSN.
  • Vaccination schedule: Follow the national RIVM schedule; most vaccines start at 6 weeks to 2 months depending on the program.

Childcare Options & Costs

Common childcare types:

  • Daycare (daycare centers / kinderdagverblijf): Full-day care for infants and toddlers; average cost 2025: €7–€9 per hour depending on region.
  • Childminder (gastouder): Home-based care with childminder, usually slightly cheaper and flexible hours.
  • Preschool / Kindergarten (peuterspeelzaal / peuterspeelgroep): Early socialization programs; subsidised places available.
  • After-school care (buitenschoolse opvang, BSO): For school-aged children outside school hours.

Childcare subsidy (Kinderopvangtoeslag)

Many parents are eligible for childcare allowance depending on income and working hours. Apply via the Belastingdienst with your DigiD once registered.

Schooling — From Primary to Secondary

  • Primary school (Basisschool): Starts at age 4–5; many international and bilingual schools exist in larger cities.
  • Secondary tracks: VMBO, HAVO, VWO — choose track based on academic ability; international schools follow IB/UK/US curricula.
  • School choice: Admissions for international schools can have waiting lists; apply early and keep local public schools as backup.

Parental Leave & Benefits

  • Maternity leave (zwangerschaps- en bevallingsverlof): Typically 16 weeks (4-6 weeks before due date + 10+ weeks after) with pay via employer or UWV depending on contract.
  • Parental leave: Shared parental leave has been expanding; check current rules with UWV and employer for pay entitlements.
  • Child benefit (kinderbijslag): Paid quarterly by SVB; apply after registration.

Practical Tips & Local Resources

  • Join local parent groups: Many municipalities and expat communities run parent-and-baby meetups — helpful for childcare recommendations.
  • Transport with children: Consider a cargo bike for short trips in cities; public transport is child-friendly but check stroller rules on trains/trams.
  • Medical emergencies: Use emergency number 112 for urgent cases; for non-urgent medical advice, contact your GP.
  • Costs: Expect childcare and housing to be the largest expenses; research local subsidies and tax benefits.
